Problem

You're on a game show. There are three doors: behind one is a car, behind the other two are goats. You pick door #1. The host opens door #3, revealing a goat. Should you switch to door #2 or stay with door #1?

Solution

You should always switch! Your initial probability of choosing correctly is 1/3. When the host reveals a goat, the probability that your original choice was correct is still 1/3, but the probability that door #2 has the car is now 2/3. So switching doubles your chances of winning!

Problem

Three guests check into a hotel. The room costs $30, so each pays $10. Later, the manager realizes the room was only $25. He gives $5 back to the bellhop to return to the guests. The bellhop keeps $2 and gives $1 to each guest. Now each guest paid $9 (total $27) plus the bellhop kept $2, totaling $29. Where's the missing dollar?

Solution

There is no missing dollar! The error is in adding the bellhop's $2 to the guests' $27. The $27 includes the bellhop's $2. The correct breakdown is: Guests paid $27 total (= $25 room + $2 tip). The math should be: $25 (room) + $2 (tip) = $27 (what guests paid).

Problem

You meet two people. One always tells the truth, one always lies. You don't know which is which. What single yes/no question can you ask one of them to find out if there's a dragon in the cave ahead?

Solution

Ask either one: "If I asked the other person if there's a dragon, would they say yes?" If there IS a dragon: the truth-teller would truthfully report that the liar would say "no", and the liar would falsely claim the truth-teller would say "no". Both would answer "no". If there is NO dragon: both would answer "yes" by the same logic. So "no" means yes, there's a dragon!

Problem

A farmer needs to cross a river with a fox, a chicken, and grain. The boat can hold only the farmer and one item. The fox eats the chicken if left alone together, and the chicken eats the grain if left alone together. How can the farmer get everything across safely?

Solution

Step-by-step solution: 1) Farmer takes chicken across (leaving fox and grain). 2) Farmer returns alone. 3) Farmer takes fox across and brings chicken BACK. 4) Farmer takes grain across. 5) Farmer returns alone. 6) Farmer takes chicken across. Success! The key is bringing the chicken back in step 3 to prevent it from being alone with the other items.
